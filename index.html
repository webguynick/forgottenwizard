The Genius

Who Was Nikola Tesla?

Born July 10, 1856 in Smiljan (modern-day Croatia), Tesla was a visionary decades ahead of his time — most of his ideas are only now being realized.

01
⚡

Father of AC Current

Tesla invented the alternating current (AC) electrical supply system — the very electricity running through your walls right now. His system won the "War of Currents" against Edison's inferior DC system and powers the modern world.

02
🌀

The Tesla Coil (1891)

In 1891, Tesla invented the resonant transformer circuit that bears his name. The Tesla Coil produces high-voltage, high-frequency alternating current — still used in radio technology, spark plugs, and entertainment to this day.

03
📡

Wireless Electricity

In Colorado Springs (1899), Tesla lit 200 lamps wirelessly from 25 miles away using Earth itself as a conductor. His Wardenclyffe Tower project aimed to transmit free electricity across the globe — a dream J.P. Morgan shut down by cutting funding.

04
📻

True Father of Radio

Tesla demonstrated radio transmission in 1893 — two years before Marconi. The U.S. Supreme Court confirmed in 1943 that Tesla's patents predated Marconi's, yet Marconi received the Nobel Prize. History misattributed the invention for decades.

05
🤖

First Remote Control

In 1898 at Madison Square Garden, Tesla demonstrated the world's first radio-controlled vehicle — a miniature boat he called a "teleautomaton." The crowd thought it was magic. He had invented remote control 125 years before smart home devices.

06
💡

Niagara Falls Harnessed

Tesla designed the first hydroelectric power plant at Niagara Falls in 1896 — the first large-scale AC electric generating station. It powered Buffalo, NY and proved that AC could deliver electricity across long distances, changing civilization forever.

07
🌩️

Man-Made Lightning

At his Colorado Springs lab, Tesla created artificial lightning bolts measuring 135 feet long — the largest man-made lightning ever produced. The discharges could be heard 15 miles away. He photographed himself reading serenely among the arcs.

08
🔬

X-Ray Pioneer

Tesla experimented with X-rays before Röntgen's famous announcement, producing high-energy X-ray photographs. He warned of their dangers and nearly had the "Shadowgraph" technology ready — but his lab burned down, destroying years of research.

09
🕊️

A Man Ahead of Time

Tesla predicted smartphones, the internet, drones, and wireless energy a century before they existed. He envisioned a "World Wireless System" that would give every person on Earth free access to power and communication — essentially what we call the internet today.

His Journey

Life & Milestones

From a small village in Croatia to the towers of New York — a life of electrifying breakthroughs.

  1. 1856

    Born in Smiljan, Austrian Empire

    Nikola Tesla is born on July 10 during a lightning storm, in the village of Smiljan (modern-day Croatia). A midwife called him "a child of the storm."

  2. 1882

    AC Motor Conceived

    While walking in a Budapest park, Tesla has a vision of a rotating magnetic field — the principle behind the AC motor. He sketches it in the dirt with a stick.

  3. 1884

    Arrives in America

    Tesla arrives in New York with four cents in his pocket, a letter of introduction to Thomas Edison, and a mind full of revolutionary ideas. He begins working at Edison's lab — but they soon clash philosophically.

  4. 1888

    AC Patents — Westinghouse Deal

    Tesla sells his AC motor and transformer patents to George Westinghouse for $60,000 — then later tears up his royalty contract to save Westinghouse from bankruptcy, forfeiting billions in future earnings.

  5. 1891

    Tesla Coil Invented · US Citizenship

    Tesla patents the Tesla Coil and becomes an American citizen. He performs spectacular demonstrations, shooting lightning through his own body to light bulbs wirelessly, astonishing audiences worldwide.

  6. 1893

    Chicago World's Fair — AC Lights the World

    Tesla and Westinghouse win the contract to power the Chicago World's Fair with AC electricity, illuminating 100,000 incandescent lamps. The display settles the War of Currents in Tesla's favor forever.

  7. 1896

    Niagara Falls Hydroelectric Plant

    Tesla's AC system powers the first industrial-scale hydroelectric plant at Niagara Falls, transmitting electricity to Buffalo, NY — 22 miles away. It's the birth of the modern power grid.

  8. 1898

    World's First Remote Control

    Tesla demonstrates a radio-controlled boat at Madison Square Garden, astonishing onlookers. He envisions radio-controlled torpedoes and autonomous machines — a century before robotics became mainstream.

  9. 1899

    Colorado Springs — Earth Resonance

    Tesla sets up a laboratory in Colorado Springs and makes what he considers his greatest discovery: that Earth itself can act as an electrical conductor. He creates lightning 135 feet long and lights 200 lamps 25 miles away wirelessly.

  10. 1901

    Wardenclyffe Tower — The Dream

    With J.P. Morgan's funding, Tesla begins constructing a 187-foot tower on Long Island designed to transmit wireless electricity and communications globally. When Morgan discovers the system would provide free power to anyone, he pulls the funding.

  11. 1915

    Nobel Prize Snub

    Rumors spread that Tesla and Edison will share the Nobel Prize. Neither receives it. Tesla, broke and exhausted, is living at the Hotel New Yorker, feeding pigeons daily — particularly one white pigeon he considers his closest companion.

  12. 1943

    Death — Legacy Begins

    Tesla dies alone in his hotel room on January 7, age 86. The U.S. Supreme Court later confirms his radio patents predated Marconi. The FBI confiscates his papers. He dies broke — yet his inventions power every home, every device, and every city on Earth.

Classified File
🔫

The Death Ray — Tesla's Most Dangerous Secret

On July 11, 1934 — his 78th birthday — Tesla called a press conference and announced something that shocked the world: he had invented a particle beam weapon capable of destroying 10,000 aircraft from 250 miles away.

He called it Teleforce. The press called it the Death Ray.

The beam would fire electrically charged particles of tungsten or mercury at 270,000 miles per hour — with 100 billion watts packed into an area smaller than a human hair. Tesla's vision was actually defensive: a ring of these weapons around a nation's coast would make war "impossible" by guaranteeing mutual destruction before any army could mobilize.

He pitched the invention to the U.S. government, the British, the French — and was turned down each time. In 1939, the Soviet government paid him $25,000 to investigate the weapon. When Tesla died in 1943, the FBI seized all his papers and placed them under military secrecy. The contents were never fully disclosed.

War of Currents
⚡

Tesla vs. Edison — The War That Lit the World

When Tesla arrived at Edison's lab in 1884, Edison told him that anyone who could redesign his DC motors would earn a $50,000 bonus. Tesla did it — and Edison laughed, saying it was "just an American joke." Tesla quit on the spot.

What followed was one of history's greatest rivalries. Edison had bet everything on Direct Current (DC) and spent years running a vicious smear campaign against Tesla's Alternating Current (AC). He publicly electrocuted animals — including Topsy the elephant — with AC power to make it look dangerous.

Tesla partnered with George Westinghouse and fought back with science. The decisive moment came at the 1893 Chicago World's Fair, where Tesla and Westinghouse lit 100,000 bulbs with AC — the first time most Americans had ever seen electric light. The War of Currents was over.

Edison never admitted defeat. In his final autobiography, he claimed his biggest regret was never pursuing AC. Tesla, for his part, tore up his royalty contract with Westinghouse to save the company from bankruptcy — forfeiting what would have been billions of dollars.

The Human Side
🕊️

The Genius and the Pigeon

In his final years, Tesla lived alone in room 3327 of the Hotel New Yorker. Broke, forgotten, and in poor health, he spent his days feeding pigeons in Bryant Park. But there was one pigeon — a white female — that he loved above all others.

"I have been feeding pigeons, thousands of them, for years," Tesla wrote. "But there was one pigeon, a beautiful bird, pure white with light grey tips on its wings… that pigeon was the joy of my life."

He nursed her back to health when she was injured, spending $2,000 (a fortune for a man with no money) on veterinary care. He believed they had a special connection. When she died, Tesla said: "When that pigeon died, something went out of my life… I knew that my life's work was finished."

The man who had electrified the world spent his last years in near total isolation. He died on January 7, 1943, alone in his hotel room. A maid found him the next morning. He was 86.

“I do not think there is any thrill that can go through the human heart like that felt by the inventor as he sees some creation of the brain unfolding to success.” — Nikola Tesla

Setting the Record Straight

Myths vs. Reality

History has been both unfair and over-romantic about Tesla. Here's what's true, what's exaggerated, and what's outright false.

❌ Myth

"Edison stole all of Tesla's ideas"

Edison and Tesla had a genuine professional falling-out over wages and working style — not intellectual theft. Their real conflict was the War of Currents: a legitimate technical and business dispute over AC vs. DC. Edison was a ruthless competitor, but calling him a thief oversimplifies a complex rivalry.

✅ True

"Marconi used Tesla's patents for radio"

This is confirmed fact. The U.S. Supreme Court ruled in 1943 — six months after Tesla's death — that Marconi's key radio patents were invalid because Tesla had filed them first. Tesla is the legal and scientific father of radio, period.

⚠️ Partially True

"Tesla discovered free energy for everyone"

Tesla did research wireless power transmission and believed electricity could be delivered cheaply at global scale. But "free energy" as perpetual motion or zero-cost power was never Tesla's claim — it was a later internet myth. His Wardenclyffe project would have been costly and required massive infrastructure. "Affordable and wireless" was the vision, not "free."

✅ True

"The government seized Tesla's papers when he died"

Confirmed. The FBI's Office of Alien Property took possession of Tesla's personal papers within hours of his death in January 1943. The papers were classified and not fully released for decades. John Trump (MIT physicist and uncle of Donald Trump) reviewed them and concluded they posed no immediate national security threat.

❌ Myth

"Tesla had no business sense at all"

Early in his career, Tesla was extremely savvy. He negotiated effectively with Westinghouse and sold his AC patents for a substantial sum. What undermined him financially was a combination of visionary projects that had no immediate commercial path (Wardenclyffe), a personality that valued discovery over profit, and genuinely bad luck with investors who prioritized short-term returns.

✅ True

"Tesla predicted smartphones and the internet"

In 1908, Tesla wrote: "As soon as [the wireless system] is completed, it will be possible for a businessman in New York to dictate instructions, and [have them] instantly appear in type at his office in London or elsewhere. He will be able to call up from his desk and talk to any telephone subscriber on the globe." That is a phone. Tesla predicted it in 1908.

Quick Answers

Frequently Asked Questions

The most common questions about Tesla — answered directly.

Why is Tesla called the "forgotten wizard"?

Despite being responsible for the AC electrical system powering every home and device on Earth, Tesla died in obscurity in 1943 — broke, alone, and largely forgotten by the scientific establishment. His rival Edison received far more popular recognition during their lifetimes. It wasn't until decades later, particularly with the rise of the internet, that Tesla's contributions were rediscovered and celebrated. The nickname "forgotten wizard" captures both his genius and his tragic erasure from mainstream history.

Did Tesla and Edison ever reconcile?

No. The two men remained at odds throughout their lives. Edison reportedly said Tesla was "the greatest inventor in the world" in private, but never publicly acknowledged his contributions. When Edison died in 1931, Tesla gave a brief statement to the press praising his energy and drive — notably without crediting his scientific work. In 1915, both men were widely reported to be sharing the Nobel Prize in Physics. Neither received it. Many historians believe mutual animosity was the reason.

How did Tesla pay for his research?

Throughout his career, Tesla relied on wealthy patrons and investors. His biggest early supporter was George Westinghouse, who purchased his AC patents. For the Wardenclyffe Tower, J.P. Morgan provided over $150,000 — then withdrew funding when he realized the system might allow anyone to access power without paying for it. Later in life Tesla struggled constantly with finances, often unable to pay his hotel bills and relying on the generosity of friends.

What were Tesla's strangest habits?

Tesla had obsessive-compulsive tendencies throughout his life. He was intensely averse to pearls — he once refused to work if a secretary wore them. He disliked touching round objects and couldn't stand hair. He was obsessed with the number 3 and would walk around a block three times before entering a building. He calculated the cubic volume of food before eating it. Despite these quirks, he was considered charming, witty, and a genuinely great dinner conversationalist — regularly dining at Delmonico's restaurant in New York.

What is the "369" theory people attribute to Tesla?

Tesla said: "If you only knew the magnificence of the 3, 6, and 9, then you would have a key to the universe." He did have a peculiar fascination with numbers divisible by 3. However, the widespread internet theory that Tesla left behind a secret code or mathematical key to free energy is not supported by historical evidence. What is real: Tesla's scientific papers, patents, and lab notebooks are extensively documented and publicly available — none contain a hidden 369 formula.

Is the modern Tesla car company connected to the real Tesla?

The company is named in his honor, and the connection is very real technically: every Tesla electric vehicle uses an AC induction motor — the specific motor technology that Nikola Tesla patented in 1888. The rotating magnetic field principle inside those motors is literally Tesla's invention, unchanged at its core. Elon Musk has stated the name was chosen deliberately as a tribute to the inventor whose work made electric motors practical.
